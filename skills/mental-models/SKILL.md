# Mental Models Checklist

Apply Charlie Munger's latticework of 35 mental models to any decision or problem. This structured multi-disciplinary analysis surfaces blind spots, biases, and second-order effects that intuitive thinking misses.

## Source Attribution

Models drawn from:
- **The Swedish Investor** YouTube series "Charlie Munger's Mental Models" (Parts 1-6, approximately 30 models)
- **Poor Charlie's Almanack** (2005, edited by Peter D. Kaufman)
- **Munger's speeches**, including the 1995 Harvard speech on the Psychology of Human Misjudgment

Part-to-model assignments are editorial. Part 7 (models 31-35) is an editorial extension — authentic Munger concepts sourced from his speeches and Poor Charlie's Almanack, but a Part 7 video has not been independently verified.

This model set was fact-checked using the veracity-tweaked-555 multi-agent audit skill (7-run convergence loop, 29 corrections applied, final score: 97/100).

## Input

`$ARGUMENTS` contains the decision or question, with an optional `--brief` flag.

Examples:

## The 35 Models

### Part 1 — Foundational Thinking Tools

**1. Inversion** (Mathematics)
Approach problems backward. Instead of asking how to succeed, ask what would guarantee failure — then avoid those things.
- Key question: *What would guarantee failure here, and how do I avoid it?*
- "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there." — Munger attributes this to "a rustic"; he popularized it, not originated it.

**2. Circle of Competence** (Philosophy)
Know the boundaries of your expertise. Operate inside your circle and expand it slowly. The danger isn't ignorance — it's the illusion of knowledge.
- Key question: *Am I operating inside my circle of competence, or fooling myself?*
- "Knowing what you don't know is more useful than being brilliant." — Widely attributed to Munger; exact occasion unconfirmed.

**3. First Principles Thinking** (Physics)
Break problems down to fundamental truths rather than reasoning by analogy. Strip away assumptions to see what's really going on.
- Key question: *What are the fundamental truths here, and what am I assuming?*

**4. Occam's Razor** (Philosophy)
Among competing hypotheses with equal explanatory power, prefer the one with the fewest assumptions. Not that the simplest answer is always right — but that unnecessary complexity should be avoided.
- Key question: *Is there a simpler explanation I'm overlooking?*

**5. Hanlon's Razor** (Psychology)
Never attribute to malice that which can be adequately explained by incompetence or ignorance (the traditional formulation uses "stupidity"; paraphrased here for generality).
- Key question: *Am I assuming bad intent when this could be explained by mistake or ignorance?*

### Part 2 — Economics & Behavioral Drivers

**6. Second-Order Thinking** (Economics)
Consider not just the immediate consequences of a decision, but the consequences of the consequences. Play out the full chain.
- Key question: *And then what? What are the second and third order effects?*

**7. Probabilistic Thinking** (Mathematics)
Think in probabilities rather than certainties. Use base rates, expected values, and Bayesian updating to make better decisions.
- Key question: *What are the base rates, and am I overweighting vivid examples?*
- "It is better to be vaguely right than exactly wrong." — Attributed to Carveth Read (1898); also used by Munger.

**8. Incentives** (Economics)
People respond to incentives — financial, social, and psychological. To understand behavior, follow the incentive structure.
- Key question: *Who benefits, and are the incentives aligned or misaligned?*
- "Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome." — Widely attributed to Munger; exact source varies.

**9. Reciprocity** (Psychology)
Humans have a deep-seated need to return favors and match behavior. This can be used ethically but also exploited.
- Key question: *Am I feeling obligated to reciprocate in a way that distorts my judgment?*

**10. Supply and Demand** (Economics)
Prices and availability are determined by the interaction of supply and demand. Understand what's scarce and what's abundant.
- Key question: *What's scarce and what's abundant in this situation?*

### Part 3 — Safety, Cost & Systems

**11. Margin of Safety** (Engineering)
Build in a buffer for being wrong. Never depend on everything going right — leave room for error, bad luck, and ignorance.
- Key question: *What's my buffer for being wrong, and can I survive the worst case?*

**12. Opportunity Cost** (Economics)
Every choice has an implicit cost — the value of the best alternative you gave up. Compare against your next-best option.
- Key question: *What am I giving up by choosing this path?*

**13. Mr. Market** (Economics)
Benjamin Graham's allegory: the market is like a manic-depressive business partner who offers to buy or sell shares at wildly varying prices. You can take advantage of his moods or ignore him. (Graham was Buffett's mentor; Munger adopted this model via Buffett.)
- Key question: *Is the crowd being rational or emotional right now?*

**14. Compound Interest** (Mathematics)
Small consistent gains accumulate exponentially over time. This applies to money, knowledge, relationships, and reputation.
- Key question: *Am I building something that compounds, or something that depletes?*
- "The first rule of compounding: never interrupt it unnecessarily." — Widely attributed to Munger; exact occasion unconfirmed.

**15. Redundancy / Backup Systems** (Engineering)
Critical systems need backups. Build in redundancy so that a single point of failure doesn't bring everything down.
- Key question: *What is the single point of failure, and do I have a backup?*

### Part 4 — Cognitive Biases & Learning

**16. Confirmation Bias** (Psychology)
The tendency to search for, interpret, and remember information that confirms your pre-existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence.
- Key question: *Am I seeking only evidence that supports what I already believe?*

**17. Social Proof** (Psychology)
People look to others' behavior to determine their own, especially under uncertainty. Powerful but often leads to herding and bubbles.
- Key question: *Am I following the crowd, or thinking independently?*

**18. Survivorship Bias** (Psychology)
We see winners and study their traits, but ignore the far larger group of losers who had the same traits. The dead don't write books.
- Key question: *Am I only seeing successes? What happened to those who tried the same and failed?*

**19. Narrative Instinct** (History)
Humans are story-telling creatures. We impose narrative on random events, seeing patterns and causation where none exists.
- Key question: *Am I creating a compelling story that isn't supported by the data?*

**20. Curiosity Instinct** (Biology)
Innate curiosity drives learning and discovery. Cultivating it across disciplines builds the latticework of mental models.
- Key question: *Am I still curious, or have I stopped learning about this domain?*

### Part 5 — Psychological Tendencies

**21. Pain-Avoiding Psychological Denial** (Psychology)
When reality is too painful, the mind distorts it. People deny uncomfortable truths rather than face them and adapt.
- Key question: *Am I avoiding an uncomfortable truth because it's painful to face?*

**22. Excessive Self-Regard Tendency** (Psychology)
People overestimate their own abilities, knowledge, and character relative to others. The endowment effect is a manifestation.
- Key question: *Am I overvaluing my own abilities or possessions?*

**23. Commitment & Consistency Bias** (Psychology)
Once committed to an idea or course of action, people resist changing course even when evidence warrants it. Sunk cost fallacy is a common manifestation.
- Key question: *Am I doubling down on a prior commitment when I should pivot?*
- "The human mind is a lot like the human egg, and the human egg has a shut-off device. When one sperm gets in, it shuts down so the next one can't get in. The human mind has a strong tendency to work the same way." — Munger, 1995 Harvard speech on the Psychology of Human Misjudgment (under "Consistency and Commitment Tendency").

**24. Authority Bias** (Psychology)
The tendency to attribute greater accuracy to the opinion of an authority figure, even outside their area of expertise.
- Key question: *Am I deferring to authority instead of thinking for myself?*

**25. Envy and Jealousy** (Psychology)
Comparing yourself to others and wanting what they have. One of the most destructive forces in human decision-making.
- Key question: *Is envy or jealousy driving this decision?*
- "The world is not driven by greed. It's driven by envy." — Munger voiced this publicly but credited the sentiment to Buffett.

### Part 6 — Systems & Scale

**26. Feedback Loops** (Engineering)
Systems produce outputs that feed back as inputs, creating positive (amplifying) or negative (stabilizing) loops. Understand which type you're dealing with.
- Key question: *Is there a feedback loop here — amplifying or stabilizing?*

**27. Equilibrium** (Economics)
Systems tend toward balance points. Understand what equilibrium looks like, what disrupts it, and what new equilibrium emerges.
- Key question: *Is this system at equilibrium, or in transition to a new one?*

**28. Critical Mass** (Physics)
Some processes require a minimum threshold before they ignite. Below critical mass, nothing happens; above it, chain reactions begin.
- Key question: *Have we reached the tipping point, or are we still below critical mass?*

**29. Advantages of Scale** (Economics)
Larger operations often enjoy lower per-unit costs, stronger brands, and more bargaining power. But scale also brings bureaucracy and complacency.
- Key question: *Does scale help or hurt in this situation?*

**30. Network Effects** (Economics)
A product or service becomes more valuable as more people use it. Creates winner-take-all dynamics and powerful moats.
- Key question: *Does this get more valuable as more people use it?*

### Part 7 — Meta-Cognitive Tools (Editorial Extension)

*Part 7 is an editorial extension. These are authentic Munger concepts sourced from his speeches and Poor Charlie's Almanack, but a Part 7 video in the Swedish Investor series has not been independently verified.*

**31. Two-Track Analysis** (Psychology)
Analyze decisions on two tracks simultaneously: (1) rational factors — what should happen based on logic and evidence, and (2) psychological factors — what subconscious biases and emotions are influencing the decision.
- Key question: *What does the rational track say, and what does the psychological track reveal?*

**32. Psychology of Human Misjudgment** (Psychology)
Munger's catalog of causes of human misjudgment — 24 in the original 1995 Harvard speech, expanded to 25 in Poor Charlie's Almanack (2005, in which Munger substantially rewrote the talk). Includes reward/punishment super-response, liking/loving tendency, disliking/hating tendency, doubt-avoidance, and inconsistency-avoidance.
- Key question: *Which of Munger's causes of human misjudgment are active here?*

**33. Checklists** (Engineering)
Systematic checklists reduce errors in complex decisions. Pilots, surgeons, and investors all benefit from forcing themselves through a structured review before acting.
- Key question: *Have I systematically checked all the important factors before deciding?*
- "No wise pilot, no matter how great his talent and experience, fails to use his checklist." — From Munger's writings on checklists; exact source varies.

**34. Lollapalooza Effect** (Psychology)
When multiple biases, tendencies, or forces combine in the same direction, the result is far larger than any single factor would predict. Extreme outcomes come from confluence, not single causes.
- Key question: *Are multiple forces combining here to create an outsized effect?*
- "The tendency to get extreme consequences from confluences of psychological tendencies acting in favor of a particular outcome." — Paraphrased from concept discussed in Poor Charlie's Almanack (2005).

**35. Chauffeur Knowledge** (Philosophy)
In a story Munger tells (widely considered apocryphal), Max Planck's chauffeur could recite the lecture perfectly but couldn't answer a single question. Distinguish between people who have real understanding and those who merely have the words.
- Key question: *Do I (or my source) have real understanding, or just chauffeur knowledge?*

## Execution Instructions

1. Parse `$ARGUMENTS`:
   - Extract the decision/question (the text content)
   - Check for `--brief` flag

2. **If `--brief`:**
   For each of the 35 models above, answer the key question in 1-2 sentences as it applies to the user's decision. If a model clearly doesn't apply, say so in one line and move on. Use numbered sections matching the model numbers. End with a 3-sentence synthesis.

3. **If full (default):**
   For each of the 35 models, organized by Part:
   - State the model name and number
   - Apply the concept to the user's specific decision
   - Answer the key question with concrete reasoning
   - Cite the Munger quote where relevant and illuminating
   - If the model doesn't meaningfully apply, say so briefly (1 line) and move on

   After all 35 models, provide a **Final Synthesis**:
   - **Summary**: What's the clear-eyed picture across all models?
   - **Recommendation**: What should the user do, and why?
   - **What would change your mind**: What new information would alter this recommendation?

4. Write with Munger's characteristic directness. Don't pad or flatter — give the hard truth. Use analogies and examples where helpful.
